I’ve been learning Japanese for approximately six months.

Well, technically I started learning the language around one and a half years ago, however I took a huge break from it during that period to pursue other endeavors, and only in the past month have I decided to take it seriously again.

It was my visit to Japan that inspired me to learn the language, and although it wasn’t always the case growing up, I can firmly say that I now have a strong passion for language learning in general. 

The reason why I wanted to write this article today is to explain why Japanese is so hard to learn and understand as a language. In part so you can better understand in layman’s terms why it’s such a bastard to learn, because it’s something that hasn’t quite become apparent to me until recently. 

And you know, now that I know why it’s difficult, it’s really helped me refine my approach towards language learning in general. Oh, and in order to do this I will compare Japanese with typical romance languages; let’s say French for the sake of this article. 

In the case of something like French, which is a relatively similar language to English, what can be insanely effective is using the Listening-Reading method which allows you to essentially blitz through the whole process; which allowed me to develop a fairly solid grasp of Italian within the space of a few days.

Ultimately it relies on utilising parallel texts along with accompanying audio in order to ingrain a language into your brain, and it’s truly fantastic. You can grasp a language very, very quickly without having to memorize a single thing.

If you’re interested in learning more, it’s detailed quite vividly in this link:

http://users.bestweb.net/~siom/martian_mountain/!%20L-R%20the%20most%20important%20passages.htm

Now, this works really well with languages which are highly similar, such as English and French, however I’ll try and explain why it doesn’t work with something like Japanese, at all. 

  • Japanese sentence and grammar structure is effectively the opposite of something like French and English, which is to say that your Japanese-English parallel texts will not match up at all; and the whole premise of the Listening-Reading method is that you can simply follow along with the text and create basic associations between the different languages. 
  • Assuming you can magically get past this hurdle without it affecting your ability to learn, it’s also phonetically impossible to actually know to pronounce a character based on it’s kanji, without also having a hiragana translation present as well. Which is to say you will need to separately learn the sound of the language phonetically, along with how to actually write it.

It honestly is that simple, which is why it doesn’t take very long to learn something like French.
