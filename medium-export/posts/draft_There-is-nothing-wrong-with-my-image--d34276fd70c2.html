There is nothing wrong with my image. 

I’m not sure if this is entirely common, however I find introspective imagery to be an entirely exhausting experience. I don’t know what it is about capturing my own physical self on film, only the amount of attrition felt within the process is clearly unhealthy. 

I immediately become self-conscious about what I look like. My hair is a fuzzy, incomprehensible mess. My facial hair can’t quite decide how much it wants to shame me and I’m still wearing the same clothes my mother decided was appropiate upon embarking puberty. 

My face looks terrible from all angles, no matter how hard I try. Most importantly, I just don’t care. It doesn’t bother me what I look like, precisely why I don’t usually take pictures of myself. 

Part of it comes down to the way in which we self-identify with myself. Each time I look at myself in a mirror, I do not see myself. The idea of who I am does not reside in what I look like.

Instead I tend to identify with the ideas, the thoughts, the feelings and emotions that run through my blood. Oh, what a wonderful realm for self-delusion to aspire to. Trust me, it ain’t pretty. 

There’s something surreal, something immaculately exciting about the world of words and logic that I find entirely inseperable from who I am. I could live entirely without music or sex or drugs, but in it’s stead is the strong connection I maintain with the intellectual beauty of my thoughts. 

I believe part of the problem is that as a species so entwined with technology, with cartoons and with ideas, we have been conditioned not to identify ourselves as human. 

Humanity is a lost cause in a world full of pornography, ideology and politics.

Our aspirations are no longer as simple as consuming pre-definable roles. The complexity in our minds laugh uncontrollably at the mential tasks we are borne to carry out on a daily basis. 

Our minds can’t handle it the triviality of existence. It crumbles into boredom. We want to be Hollywood celebrities, imaginary sex gods, other kin; highly exaggerated constructions which could not possibly exist in our human bodies. 

Mental illness. You’ve done it again.
