How We Make Comedy — Part 2: The Returns

I am not entirely sure what prompted me to start producing videos again.

The thought had not arisen in more than two years and my focus was elsewhere; on my career, my relationship, my aspirations of becoming a competent coder.

Admittedly I had left the channel on a bad note and certainly, I knew it was not making me any happier as an individual.

So I stopped.

Part of me felt like it was in my best interest to quit the conundrum. I knew my mind could not cope with the incessant self-depreciation and dicking around; all those petty notions of popularity and fame that had forced me into a box of assumptions so large, that it crushed me as an individual and destroyed me as a soul.

Well, to claim destruction is a little a harsh. Rather, it fragmented me into an even more jaded and religiously hateful person.

Arguably, I was too young and immature to embrace the responsibility of performing comedy; that fine-line between joking around and truly believing that you, the humble punchline, was nothing more than a sacrificial lamb for the masses to gouge at.

While I did not quite know it, I was mentally ill at the time.

Naturally, growing up in extraordinarily cynical and malevolent circumstances from a young age will lead you to believe that everyone else is ruining your life, rather than yourself.

While I relied on my comedy as a personal expression of the madness uttering my mind, it was largely an expression that dug itself a little too deeply within the entrenched sod of my downtrodden nature.

I was an addicted, depressed, judgemental piece of crap.

Of course, that was hardly the reason why I stopped making videos.

When you are mentally ill, you remain so eternally blind to your own flaws and reason that you illegibly chase the next hapful decision like a rat approaching poison in the middle of a disguised elixir.

I had decided instead to focus on losing my virginity because “that was the real reason I was so fucked up”.

As you can imagine, this path in life merely developed into an even greater insecurity about who I was and eventually after a terrible string of dainty relationships, I decided it was best to focus on the cause of my mental illness, rather than the repetitive symptoms haunting me daily in the obsessiveness of my actions.

Well, it has been more than two years of shameless recovery and I am actually feeling quite good about myself.

It is like I have tricked my mind into being happy. Rather brilliant, I say.

I am now lucky enough to have a full-time job in a career that my tattered journalism degree is entirely undeserving of. I have an amazing girlfriend whom I want to spend the rest of my life with and I really am incredibly grateful for simply being alive.

…and to think, I did not even need to pop a pill to get through it all (though actually, I was obsessively taking acid at the time, in the form of tabs).

Back in 2013, in the depths of my silly mind, I was under the impression that the channel was going nowhere.

I now realise, here in 2015, that I had actually squandered a wonderful opportunity.

However, I have zero regrets what-so-ever of the decisions I have made in my life. Taking that time off was the best thing I ever did for myself and I am eternally grateful to have had the courage to let go and leave my former abusive relationship with myself.

I suppose I want to prove to myself that I am truly capable of achieving my goals and go against this notion repeated failure can ever define an individual. There is always opportunity lurking around the corner and it is a bright perspective for those who want it.

In fact, the reason why I am giving it another crack is because I know I have the talent to succeed. I know what I am capable of and comedy is truly the one talent where I can dominate and express myself clearly.

The only difference is that I need to actually need to stick with it this time, rather than allow the self-doubt to overcome me.

Interestingly enough, there was very little I enjoyed about making videos. I hated filming; arguably one of the most tedious tasks available. I hated acting; to an extent, and editing was okayish.

What really shined however, like a bright beating angel in my heart, was the final product; something which I could call my own and feel proud of.

It makes me really, quite happy.

If you must know, I have always been somewhat obsessed with media. If I was not making videos, then I was producing podcasts or murmuring stupid voices to myself like a chatterbox for the queazy.

I can always tell how in-love I am with my media depending on the number of times I re-watch or re-listen to the thing I have produced. It does not matter if it is something I have written or performed; I have this habit of consuming it over and over again for days at a time and I always find it striking to discover something new with each iteration.

It reminds me of when I was starting out as a quack in community radio, avid as any 16 year old possibly could be.

After each show I would try to get home as quickly as possible so that I could edit the audio and listen back to the show.

I would then proceed to listen to the week’s show on repeat until I was able to broadcast again for that same terrible hour, to which I would obsessively listen back again and again and again.

Perhaps you cannot relate.

I do not blame you for thinking I am insane.

These recent events have made me realise that there has been something missing from my dear life; something which once kept me up at night for hours at a time.

That missing element was passion, and to experience this kind of passion again is the most blissful I have felt in a long while.

To feel so compelled to do something; to a point that you are willing to spend hours a day perfecting the art with your every breath, is a privilege that very few people have to experience and it’s something I am wholly grateful for.

I feel like a new man.

If it is one thing I have learnt from my departure, something which I now deeply respect and value, it is that there is no substitute for hard work — something which my mental illness abused endlessly.

While I worked hard on my comedy, very rarely was I consistent. In-between videos I would fall into deep depressions. I would be rendered useless as the voices in my mind told me how worthless and pointless I was as an individual, relative to the disappointing waste of time that was my comedy.

Now-a-days however, I consider myself a confident, patient, hard-working individual and perhaps that is why I am so excited to be making videos again. I have aspirations of becoming big and I feel like nothing can stop me.

Above all, I have also come to realise that there is nothing more important that having sufficient mental health in life, no matter what your aspirations.

Without the ability to draw perspective from your environment, you are literally helpless and without the ability to control your mind, you may as well be virtually dead.

I am not sure what prompted me to start experimenting with the camera again, but like a dam full of passion, the barriers just cratered below and my mind has become fixated on writing scripts and studying comedy since.

While away on a business trip, I decided to make a pilot episode and it was an absolutely joyous experience. I probably watched the video more than 100 hundred times back-to-back, over and over again.

I have decided to write these posts so that I can document and explore this side of my personality that I am incredibly fond of. Sure, I am no genius, however hopefully with time, I can become something remarkable.

A few weeks earlier, I decided to upload my first video in more than two years and with it came a rushing joy of euphoria and pride. My child-like senses finally returned and I was reminded of how much it all meant to me.

During my absence I had allowed myself to be convinced that I wasn’t good enough. Certainly, there were a lot of factors that contributed to my negative state of mind, however these are things that are completely behind me.

No amount of torment is worthy of your attention.

Most importantly, I believe comedy is an integral part of learning to be comfortable with myself and who I am. Deep down inside, I am just doing what makes me happy and it is something I am learning not to feel ashamed of, nor feel any less of myself for it.

This is my mental health strategy and arguably, there is no better cure than art and laughter.
