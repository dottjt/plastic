However there’s something about my ambition that has me firmly worried, as if my throat has become curiously inadequate for the prying hands refusing to let go of my dangerously weak neck.

That tight grip of painful disarray has me laughing deliriously, yet I am not choking.

The brain snaps. The body readjusts.

I have rightfully been lead to believe that my feelings are worthless, and perhaps I am correct.

I know it’s because I hardly feel anything at all, yet it’s what helps me envision my place in society. I mean, you can almost taste the grandeur permeating through my digital byline, thick lines forming yanky around the modules decomposing pound-cake through the subtle reposition of your neck.

It’s a snake with deformed hands. It whispers softly:

“You are one of us.”

I see right through you, puck.

Obviously, I am correct in the hollow deception I carry like a burden without forethought. For I am Asian Pierce Brosnan… with an exquisitely fruity twist.

You see, I’m a young person. And like a lot of young people, having a job is no longer a luxury most of us can afford.

Instead we are taught from a young age that jobs are inherently canabalistic; this kind of feral beast that merely subsists amongst the various other scoundrel, deliberating it’s existence along the mouldy crust we congratulate as decorative aspartame.

Yummy, yummy, so very delicieux — if it weren’t for your debt collector who knows more about your life than you do it.

For the lucky few of us with our weary choices behind us, we have these wholesome and inappropriately titled substitute-things called ‘careers’, which are much more black and sinister than any amount of lecturing on the ethics of free-will ever could be.

With a career you no longer become confined to the petty separation between work, life and leisure.

Instead it all blurs into a disgusting husk of pre-worn fabric, flouted without dopamine or sex appeal — a candid reminder that your body just ain’t up for the 5:50am sludge through the dissipating remains of a rotting corpse.

It moves with the motion that carries you towards your deathbed.

Under your career you now become subject to a wide range of exhaustive abuse that you learn to love and cherish.

It’s almost, almost.

The march without the chant. A lossless conspiracy that consumes as much as it preaches. I’ve become so absolutely encompassed by my application, to a point where it’s no longer considered polite to both breathe and smile at the same time. I barely even know who I am anymore.

The scarcity is beautiful, they say.

And I would agree.

On the other hand, this is what I’ve been lead to idolise and the tricky thing about it all is that unlike a lot of my peers, I’m actually surviving.

Because I urge you to please not be mistaken. This is purely survival we’re talking about here and I will rip another man’s jaw off if it means my table will shine brighter than the table next to me, when my soul becomes available for auction to the next highest available bidder.

After all, this is mental health on steroids.

My purpose is nothing more than pure, unadulterated, honest ambition — transpiring itself into a modal tune of frequent exercise, healthy reflection and wistful charity, because I am the moral equivalent of a Bolivian trust fund.

What I am doing is right.

It’s what the Government wants. It’s what society wants. It’s what my parents probably would have wanted, given the amount of drugs I’ve taken and laundered, to despise what the anti-careerists have been meandered for decades.

Hope.

And my gosh, have I never been more hopeful in my entire life, as I crack open a beer and glass my wife.

I suppose there’s a very broken quality to my personality that both elaborates itself into the sickness permeating through my brain, as well as through the kind, neurological trickle, delegating it’s frittle rub against my premonition, like a prince gallantly marching towards war.

No, no.

It’s not amnesia.

There’s nothing inappropriately sexy about forgetting how to tie your shoelaces, and certainly there’s a stature being held back here that wants to rip my face off and surround me with resounding empathy.

My problem is that I’m greedy with hope and ambition, and I don’t know when to stop.

Because when I want something, I work hard at it, and I don’t stop until I have it.

I’m blinded by the journey and I couldn’t imagine it any other way.

*places gun to head*
