How friendship has become an interface. How the internet changed friendship into an interface.

One of the great things about the internet is that it has lowered the barrier for a wide range of things that were previously unobtainable or sparse. Quite happily, the internet is a place for information to flourish and ideas to be shared; a global platform for eliminating attrition.

With a rudimentary knowledge of Google, anyone can just about locate anything they desire and platforms such as Twitter and Facebook, which have become ubiquitous within most our lives, never has communication become more effortless.

On the internet, friends come and go like they never existed.

When someone likes your post, they automatically become your friend for that small moment in time.

When you read someone’s article on their website, it feels as if you’re reading the words of a friend. They are sharing knowledge with you on an intimate level on their own platform.

In the past you would have established authorities such as newspapers, government and radio stations that would be channels of communications for us to understand the world.

Now these are simple relics of the past and with millions of different platforms to channel our own message, ideas of friendship, truth and belief are no longer sacred.

On a more fundamental level, I believe it exposes the subjugated meaning of what friendship has become (or perhaps, always was).

It seems, especially considering where the internet is going, that friendship is now largely a feeling, rather than any kind of integral bond held together by ideas of loyalty or honour.
