2 Introducing abstraction.

Terms such as ‘mental illnesses, ‘mental health’ and ‘perspective’ should not be thought of as concrete, tangible concepts. They are merely ideas and conventions which are vague abstractions, created and defined within the context of this guide.

A large part of our mental illness relates to the way we interpret and value words. In our dysfunction and mental illness, we misconstrue the role of words as concepts and representations of ideas, rather than as a source of truth.

While the word ‘cat’ relates to an animal in our mind, the word ‘cat’ is merely an abstraction of the black lines you are currently viewing on your screen. This is to say that our words, much like our qualities and descriptions, are entirely self-created.

The importance of abstraction.

This distinction is useful within the context of mental illness, as mental illness itself is an abstraction of the ideas and conventions we have of reality.

To begin our journey, we’ll be constructing a basic understanding of the conscious mind, its strengths and limitations, as well as the ways in which we either intentionally or unintentionally misuse the conscious mind to develop mental illness.

What is the conscious mind.

The conscious mind is an internal mechanism which dictates the level of awareness we have on our emotions, thoughts and general senses. In other words, the conscious mind plays a large part in how we remain aware of the world around us, and additionally, how we perceive reality.

We can use the conscious mind to become more or less aware of reality by using our focus and attention. This ability to remain focused is affected by a number of different factors, in particular, the state of our mental health.

In many ways, it can help to think of the conscious mind as a cursor we can control on a computer screen. With this cursor, we can change to interact with our memories, our thoughts, our emotions and any other elements within our mind.

Limitations of the conscious mind.

It is also important to outline the limitations of the conscious mind, which is to say that we cannot consciously use our minds to fly. More crucially, that the conscious mind can only function within the present moment.

This often contradicts with the way we feel, especially in relation to our memories. Depending on where we place our awareness through our focus and attention, it can feel as if we are reliving moments from our past.

Often there are natural cases where we cannot maintain control over our conscious mind through our focus and attention. This occurs when we are feeling tired, or perhaps when we are feeling alert and excited. These aspects are also affected by our diet, along with a number of different things.

Conscious mind actively creates reality.

While the conscious mind may only seem like a passive magnifying glass, allowing us to utilise our focus and attention to change our awareness, in reality, our awareness is the only mechanism we have to process reality.

This is to say that the conscious mind plays a fundamental and very active role in creating reality, rather than something which is merely exposed to the world around us. It also emphasises the power of the individual to change the way they think, feel and perceive the world.

How the conscious mind has evolved.

It can help to look back at how the human brain has developed evolutionarily over time, to get a better sense of how our conscious control came about.

In the beginning there was the reptilian brain, which dictated basic function such as survival instinct and other primitive responses. The mammalian brain began to develop with the arrival of the first mammals, allowing us to record memories and develop unique behaviours depending on our emotions. Lastly, the neocortex developed is the latest development of the brain present in humans, which is involved with higher functions such as sensory perception, spatial reasoning, conscious thought and language.

While consciousness is an aspect that involves the entire brain as a whole, aspects of our consciousness such as our self-awareness rely on many functions contained within the neocortex. Functions such as planning, introspection, learning, abstraction and developing beliefs.

These are all core functions which essentially make us human; functions which other species lack or have a limited capacity to reason with. Often in neglecting the conscious mind, we end up consulting with the mammalian or reptilian brain, to dictate how we live our lives.

How we revert to devolution of the conscious mind.

Rather than using our conscious mind to simply act on our intentions, we instead use fear and emotional manipulation to try to dictate the way we think, feel and behave. We use fear and reward to motivate and condition ourselves. It is something will explore later.

Furthermore, the modern reality of the 21st century does not allow us to neglect the neocortex, otherwise we’d be neglecting the thousands of years of civilization that we created with it. In fact, we call people who abuse or neglect the neocortex as being mentally ill; incompatible with the society we live in.

How the conscious mind relates to mental illness.

“Mental illness” is a term we prescribe to severe episodes of mental dysfunction, because we lose such a level of conscious control over ourselves, that it severely hinders our ability to dictate any mental function required to sustain ourselves in the modern world.

Mental illness is as much a state of mind, as it is a function, which limits our conscious experience to focus only on our feelings of trauma, obsession, hopelessness and emotional pain, whether real or perceived.

It makes us feel hopeless and incapable, sometimes believing that we deserve and that these feelings have been projected onto us, which is what happens when we focus on our feelings, emotions and even thoughts to delegate our reality.

Of course, it is not that we are hopeless or incapable. It is simply that we are unaware of our ability to think consciously of ourselves. It is like trying to use a computer with the mouse disconnected. The system will continue running, however it is running independent of your own intention.

What is mental illness?

Mental illness is an incredibly personal experience that will affect almost everyone at some point in their lives.

For some people, mental illness can derive from the death of a loved one, while for others it can be an obsession over past trauma resulting in OCD, anxiety and the thousands of potential symptoms associated with mental illness.

Mental illness for some individuals revolves around the array of expectations they have of how smart, rich, thin or pretty they should be, in the process adopting a backdrop of delusional self-images of hopelessness or grandeur severely devoid from reality.

In a large portion of cases, mental illness arrives from trying to keep up with the pressures of the 21st century, rather than from trauma, abuse or neglect. Many of us struggle with the stress of work and even the constant influx of information which we receive from the internet.

Mental illness can be as simple as developing a number of dysfunctional cognitive behaviours which have been cognitively reinforced throughout the years, without any significant cause or reason; usually due to simply being unaware of the consequences of our actions.

Then there are those few individuals who already have a strong grasp of their mind and hope to develop methods which allow them to study ten hours each day without experiencing mental fatigue or dysfunction: pushing their mental abilities to the extreme.

Regardless of why you’re here or what you may be struggling with, we all have one goal in mind: We are all trying to understand how our minds work so that we can overcome the perceived and real challenges which haunt us on a daily basis.
